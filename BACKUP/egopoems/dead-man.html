Dead Man

The building blared and
screamed fire into
the early Sunday morning,
the part of morning that is
still considered
night. It screamed at
nothing and we groaned
at nothing as we
completed our
exodus reluctantly.

Then we found him there,
a blue tie loose around his
neck like a badly made
hangman's noose,
browned white shirt,
dusty dress pants.
There was something poetic about
the way he lay there--
perfectly and peacefully,
straight from head to toe,
arms at the side,
like a dead man,
a casualty in a skirmish.
And he continued to lay there
dead-like,
on the concrete stairs,
his head in the bushes,
wet leaves up to his eyes,
while two of us touched his wrists
in search of life.
The rest of us stared
and wondered
if this was the fire.

But he moved,
shook his head under the leaves,
and that was enough
to let us know
he only looked dead.
So they carried him away
in his perfect form,
the noose still around his neck.
back.
